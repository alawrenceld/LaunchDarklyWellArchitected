# Regulated Industries — Design Principles

These principles extend the LDWA [General Design Principles](../../framework/design-principles.md) and the pillar-level design principles for regulated workloads.

---

## RI-1. Separation of duties is encoded, not socialized

The engineer who proposes a production change is not the engineer who approves it. This is a hard rule, not a guideline. It is enforced by configuration — Guardian Edition required approvals, pipeline gates, custom roles — not by hallway conversations.

## RI-2. Every change has an attributable actor and a documented rationale

Each change in the audit log is tied to a real, named individual (no shared accounts) and carries a description explaining *why* the change was made. Empty change descriptions are treated as policy violations.

## RI-3. The compliance scope of each system is explicit

For every LD-managed system, the team can name the compliance regimes that apply: which controls, which evidence is required, which auditor's report it ends up in. Compliance scope is per system, not per company.

## RI-4. Federal-scoped workloads run on the offering designed for them

If your workload is subject to FedRAMP, ITAR, or comparable high-assurance regimes, deploy it on [LaunchDarkly Federal](https://launchdarkly.com/docs/home/infrastructure/federal). Don't try to wrestle the commercial offering into compliance — the boundaries, the threat model, and the audit expectations are different.

## RI-5. Evidence is continuous, not episodic

Compliance evidence is generated by the same systems that operate the workload — the audit log, the approval records, the access reviews, the change history. The auditor asks; the team queries; the evidence is produced. No frantic reconstruction.

## RI-6. The residency and data-handling boundary is unambiguous

For workloads under residency requirements (GDPR, country-specific, regional contractual obligations), the team can draw the data-flow boundary, point to the controls that enforce it, and demonstrate that flag evaluation honors it.

## RI-7. Change windows and freezes are enforced, not advised

Regulated industries operate under change windows (no production changes during quarter close, during regulatory reporting periods, during certified maintenance windows). Those windows are encoded in tooling, not memorized by individuals.

## RI-8. The auditor's questions are answered by reading the system, not interviewing engineers

When the auditor asks "show me the last 90 days of production changes to flags tagged as SOX-relevant," the team produces the export. When they ask "show me who has access to billing-flow flags in production," the team produces the export. The system is the source of truth.

## RI-9. Regulatory change cycles are tracked deliberately

Regulations evolve (PCI-DSS revisions, FedRAMP control updates, EU AI Act phased applicability). The team tracks the cycle and updates LDWA controls (custom roles, required approvals, audit queries) when the regime changes.

## RI-10. The risk register includes LaunchDarkly

The team's enterprise risk register names LaunchDarkly as a system in scope, identifies the residual risks, and ties them to controls. LaunchDarkly is not an invisible dependency.
